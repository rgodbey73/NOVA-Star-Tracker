WHAT IS NOVA?
NOVA is a synthetic star tracker demonstration. It simulates how a real spacecraft camera identifies its pointing direction — RA, Dec, and Roll — purely by matching the star pattern it sees against a built-in catalog of ~120 named stars.
WHAT IS A STAR TRACKER?
A star tracker is a camera on a spacecraft that takes pictures of the sky and uses the star pattern to determine which way the spacecraft is pointing, without any GPS or ground contact. It's one of the most precise sensors ever built — accurate to a fraction of an arcsecond.
WHAT IS A QUATERNION?
A quaternion is four numbers that describe a 3D rotation — like a compass heading but for all three axes at once. A star tracker outputs one every frame to tell the spacecraft exactly how it's oriented in space.
w
A "how much rotation" number, always between 0 and 1 (we force w≥0). Close to 1 means the camera is near its reference direction (RA=0°, Dec=0°). As it rotates further away, w decreases toward 0. Works together with x, y, z — rarely interpreted alone.
x
Rotation axis toward the vernal equinox (RA = 0h).
y
Rotation axis toward RA = 6h on the celestial equator.
z
Rotation axis toward the north celestial pole.
WHAT IS AN ARCSECOND?
A degree is divided into 60 arcminutes, and each arcminute into 60 arcseconds — so one arcsecond is 1/3600th of a degree. At arm's length, a single arcsecond is roughly the width of a human hair. Star trackers routinely achieve accuracy of 5–30 arcseconds, meaning they can pinpoint a spacecraft's orientation to a fraction of a human hair's width as seen from across a room. For reference, the full Moon spans about 1,800 arcseconds across.
